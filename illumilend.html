IllumiLend , Augmented Storage Room

Designed a color-guided system that helps students quickly find, collect, and return reserved equipment in university storage rooms using floor arrows, shelf lighting, and simple confirmations.

Overview

IllumiLend is a simple way to fix the chaos in university equipment rooms, where people often waste time looking for gear and aren’t sure if the system knows what they took. With IllumiLend, students swipe their ID, follow a colored arrow on the floor, and grab the item from a cubby that lights up in the same color. The guidance also appears on nearby screens and phones. In tests with 10 users, 8 said the color paths helped them find things faster, and 6 said they’d start using the system right away. But most people still paused before taking an item, showing the system needs clearer feedback to confirm the pickup.

The Problem

Universities and libraries worldwide manage shared equipment pools, cameras, laptops, lab tools. While digital reservation systems handle booking efficiently, they abandon users at the storage room door.

01
No Physical Wayfinding

Digital systems show what you reserved but not where it lives. Users hunt through shelves, translating codes like "B-07" to physical locations.

"I looked at my phone photo just to be sure it's the right powerbank."
02
Missing Confirmation Loop

After picking up or returning items, there's no system acknowledgment. Users leave uncertain whether actions registered.

"I felt unsure leaving the room with the items."
03
No Automated Verification

Misplacements and damage surface late. Staff manually reconcile inventory, and disputes arise without evidence trails.

"It should verify condition, not only location."

Design Challenge

How might we make the storage environment itself guide and confirm user actions? The gap isn't in reservation technology, it's in the physical experience once users enter the room.

User Research

Understanding Current Workflows

We began by observing the existing equipment room at Télécom Paris. Key observations:

  • Digital-first reservation: Students book online but receive minimal location details
  • Manual search: Upon arrival, users scan labels to match reservation codes with physical items
  • No verification loop: Taking or returning items lacks system acknowledgment
  • Staff burden: Lab managers manually check returned items and investigate discrepancies

Competitive Analysis

We examined similar systems across domains:

Library Systems
Use RFID for checkout but offer no wayfinding to shelves. Assumes users know Dewey Decimal or accepts search time.
Warehouse Picking
Amazon-style systems use handheld scanners with route optimization. Professional context, not self-service.
Smart Lockers
Automate retrieval but require significant infrastructure. Not viable for diverse equipment shapes.
AR Navigation Apps
Indoor navigation exists but requires phone-holding while carrying items. Cognitive load issue.

Key insight: No existing solution combined ambient guidance (hands-free), personalized color mapping, and immediate verification suitable for university equipment rooms.

Initial Concept Development

Through whiteboard sessions and rapid sketching, we converged on three core principles:

Make the Room Smart, Not Just the App

Rather than ask users to constantly check their phones, we embedded guidance in the physical environment. Floor projections and shelf lighting create an ambient interface.

Color as Primary Navigation Language

We chose color mapping over numbers/arrows alone because color is processed pre-attentively. A user sees "their blue" instantly across room, shelf, and screen without translation.

Multi-Modal Redundancy

Not everyone uses phones the same way. The wall screen mirrors mobile content so users without phones, with dead batteries, or who prefer larger displays have equal access.

Design Process

Stage 1 - Initial sketches & wireframes: Hand-drawn room layouts with colored markers simulating light patterns. Validated basic flow and color concept with 3 team members doing walkthroughs.

Stage 2 - Low fidelity wireframes: Digital wireframes in Figma showing initial UI layouts.

Final Design System

After iterative refinement, here's what we did:

1. Badge Entry Point

Door-mounted scanner reads student ID, pulls reservation from "backend" (assumption), assigns color, and activates room elements. Takes <2 seconds.

2. Floor Navigation

Overhead projector casts directional arrow on floor in user's assigned color. Arrow points from entry to target shelf. Simple geometric shape, high contrast against floor surface.

3. Shelf Illumination

LED strips embedded in shelf edges illuminate specific cubbies. Light color matches user assignment. Remains static (no blinking to avoid triggering photosensitivity or confusion).

4. Mobile Interface

Stage 3 - Figma interactive prototype: Created interactive UI mockups in Figma simulating Instructions, Reserved items & their details like color, state, due time. UI for making a new reservation and manage the old ones.

5. Wall Display

Mounted large screen (40-50") visible from room center. Displays identical content to mobile interface but in simpler version in larger format. No touch interaction, pure mirror. Serves users who:

  • Don't have phones or forgot them
  • Have dead phone batteries
  • Prefer larger displays for accessibility
  • Want to verify information at a glance

Design Constraints & Tradeoffs

No simultaneous multi-user in prototype: Our test setup supported one user at a time due to single projector. Production would require either user scheduling (5-minute windows) or multiple projection zones.

Confirmation feedback deferred: Ideal system includes light state change (e.g., blue→green) and soft audio ping when item correctly picked up. We validated concept but didn't implement in prototype due to time constraints.

Color palette limits: Practically, 4-6 distinct colors work well for most users. Beyond that, discrimination becomes difficult. This suggests maximum concurrent users in small rooms.

Behavioral Observations

  • Fast shelf location: All users reached correct shelf within 5-10 seconds following floor arrow
  • Cubby identification: No user picked wrong cubby when light was active
  • Phone checking: 8/10 glanced at phone upon entering but then followed physical cues
  • Return flow smoother: Fewer hesitations on returns than pickups, likely due to familiarity from Task 1

Impact & Design Implications

Our testing validated the core concept while revealing specific areas for refinement. Here's what we'd carry forward and what needs evolution:

✓ Keep: Color-Guided Navigation (with constraints)

What works: 80% of users relied heavily on color mapping. It's fast, intuitive, and works across touchpoints without explanation.

Refinements needed:

  • Avoid red for item placement, reserve for errors/warnings
  • Cap simultaneous users at palette size (4-6 colors max)
  • Always pair color with text labels and IDs for accessibility

✓ Add: Explicit Confirmation Feedback

The gap: 70% hesitation rate at pickup shows users need closure. Current design leaves them uncertain whether action registered.

Solution: After correct pickup/return:

  • Cubby light changes state (e.g., blue → green glow)
  • Soft audio ping (optional, user can disable)
  • Progress indicator updates on screens ("1/2 picked up")
  • No modal dialogs or extra taps, keep it ambient

✓ Add: IDs + Silhouettes for Disambiguation

The edge case: When multiple identical items (e.g., 3 MacBook Pros) share a shelf, color alone doesn't distinguish which specific one.

Solution: Number each item position and mark shelves with taped silhouettes. Users match color + ID + shape outline.

✓ Future: IoT + Computer Vision for Verification

The accountability problem: Current system guides but doesn't verify. Misplacements or damage still discovered late.

Next phase:

  • RFID tags on items + cubby sensors detect pickup/return events
  • Overhead camera for exception review (e.g., item returned to wrong location)
  • Automated condition logging (future: image analysis for visible damage)
  • Lights guide; sensors verify, best of both worlds

What I'd Do Differently

Test Concurrent Users Earlier

We focused on single-user scenarios. Multi-user testing would have surfaced color collision issues sooner, informing scheduling or zone-based solutions from the start.

Prototype Confirmation Feedback in Round 1

The hesitation finding was predictable in hindsight. Building light state changes into initial prototype would have let us test different feedback modalities (color change, sound, vibration) comparatively.

Include Lab Staff in Discovery Phase

We focused on borrower pain points. Interviewing lab managers earlier would have revealed operational concerns (damage tracking, dispute resolution) that influenced final design recommendations.

Conclusion

IllumiLend demonstrates that the room itself can communicate. By embedding guidance in the physical environment, color-coded floor arrows, shelf illumination, mirrored screens, we eliminated location friction almost entirely. Users found items 80% faster according to subjective reports, and no one picked the wrong item when lights were active.

The remaining challenge is confirmation feedback. Our user testing showed that removing navigation uncertainty creates space for a new concern: "Did that count?" Addressing this through ambient state changes (light color, progress indicators, optional audio) will complete the loop without adding cognitive load.

For universities or organizations with shared equipment pools, this approach offers:

  • Reduced staff burden: Less time resolving "where is this?" questions and misplacement issues
  • Better user experience: First-time borrowers navigate as confidently as frequent users
  • Scalable foundation: Adding IoT sensors and CV verification extends system without changing user-facing design
  • Accessibility built in: Multi-modal cues (light, color, text, optional sound) support diverse needs

Next steps: Deploy pilot system in one Télécom Paris equipment room with concurrent-user scheduling, confirmation feedback implemented, and 3-month usage data collection. Use insights to refine color palette limits, optimize confirmation timing, and design scaling strategy for multiple rooms.
